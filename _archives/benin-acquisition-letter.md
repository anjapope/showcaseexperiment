This internal museum correspondence discusses the acquisition of ivory objects from the Benin Punitive Expedition of 1897, providing important provenance documentation for objects now subject to repatriation discussions.

## Document Context

Following the British military expedition to Benin City in February 1897, thousands of objects including ivory carvings, bronze plaques, and other items were dispersed through official and unofficial channels. This letter documents discussions about acquiring pieces for the museum collection.

## Significance

The document reveals:

- The speed with which objects moved from military hands to museum collections
- Contemporary attitudes toward acquisition of colonial materials
- The specific pieces being discussed and their described condition
- Prices paid and negotiations with intermediaries

## Contemporary Relevance

Documents like this are crucial for ongoing repatriation discussions. They establish clear provenance linking specific objects to the 1897 expedition, providing the documentary evidence needed for ethical review of colonial-era acquisitions.

## Research Notes

This letter is part of a larger archive of correspondence related to Benin materials. Cross-referencing with inventory records and photographs allows researchers to trace the journey of specific objects from Benin to their current locations.
